⚠️ Critical Action Required: HLH is a rapidly progressive, life-threatening condition. Treatment should not be delayed for diagnostic confirmation. Time to treatment initiation target: <6 hours from diagnosis.

Overview & General Principles

Treatment Objectives

Immediate: Control life-threatening hyperinflammation
Short-term: Prevent organ damage and death
Long-term: Address underlying triggers and achieve cure when possible

Risk Stratification

Mild HLH/MAS

Limited organ dysfunction with stable vital signs

Moderate HLH/MAS

Evidence of organ dysfunction (SOFA score 2+ in non-hematologic systems)

Severe HLH/MAS

Life-threatening organ failure, need for organ support, CNS involvement

Diagnostic Approach

Clinical Suspicion Triggers

  • Sepsis-like illness unresponsive to appropriate therapy
  • Unexplained cytopenias with hepatosplenomegaly
  • Ferritin >500 μg/L (>10,000 μg/L highly suspicious)
  • Fever with coagulopathy in rheumatologic patients
Key Finding: Ferritin >10,000 μg/L has 96% specificity and 90% sensitivity for HLH (Henter, Pediatric Blood & Cancer 2008)

Rapid Diagnostic Workup

Initial Panel (Obtain within 4 hours)

  • Complete blood count with differential
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel
  • Ferritin (with dilutions if >10,000)
  • Fibrinogen, triglycerides
  • LDH, AST/ALT, bilirubin
  • Soluble CD25 (if available)

Secondary Testing (Within 24 hours)

  • Bone marrow aspirate for hemophagocytosis
  • Imaging for hepatosplenomegaly
  • Flow cytometry for NK cell function (pediatric patients)
  • Genetic testing panel (if primary HLH suspected)

Diagnostic Goal: <24 hours from suspicion

Treatment Protocols

Supportive Care (All Patients)

  • ICU monitoring for moderate-severe cases
  • Infection control: Broad-spectrum antibiotics if febrile/neutropenic
  • Organ support: Mechanical ventilation, vasopressors, RRT as needed
  • Blood products: Maintain Hgb >7 g/dL, platelets >20K (>50K if bleeding)
  • Nutritional support: Early enteral nutrition when feasible

Primary HLH Treatment

Standard Protocol (HLH-2004 Based)

Etoposide 150 mg/m² IV twice weekly × 2 weeks, then weekly
Rationale: Selectively depletes activated CD8+ T cells that drive cytokine storm. Pre-HSCT survival improved from 73% (HLH-94) to 81% (HLH-2004). 5-year survival of 59% (Bergsten et al., Blood 2017).
Dexamethasone 10 mg/m²/day divided q12h × 2 weeks, then taper
Rationale: Provides immediate anti-inflammatory effect and superior CNS penetration (critical given 30-73% of pediatric cases have neurologic involvement).
Cyclosporine Target level 200-400 ng/mL
Rationale: Targets T-cell activation through calcineurin inhibition, provides sustained immunosuppression to prevent reactivation.

Alternative Agents

Emapalumab (if etoposide contraindicated) 1 mg/kg IV twice weekly, increase to 3-10 mg/kg based on response
Evidence: 70% overall response rate in refractory/relapsed patients (Locatelli et al., NEJM 2020). However, only 41% survived without additional chemotherapy. Most patients still require additional immunosuppression. Higher cost requires careful consideration.
Alemtuzumab (experienced centers only) 10 mg/m² IV 3× weekly
Use for: Severe refractory cases. Provides potent lymphocyte depletion with rapid onset. Requires experienced center due to significant immunosuppression risk.

CNS Involvement

  • High-dose dexamethasone: 10-20 mg/m²/day
  • Intrathecal therapy: Methotrexate 12-15 mg IT weekly
  • Consider intensified systemic therapy

Secondary HLH Treatment

Infection-Associated HLH

Mild-Moderate Cases:
  • Treat underlying infection aggressively
  • Dexamethasone: 0.5-1 mg/kg/day × 3-5 days
  • IVIG: 1-2 g/kg divided over 2-5 days
Severe Cases:
  • Add Etoposide: 100 mg/m² weekly (adults), 75 mg/m² (elderly)
  • Anakinra: 2-10 mg/kg/day IV/SC (particularly for sepsis-associated HLH)
Anakinra Rationale: Targets IL-1β elevation in sepsis-associated hyperinflammation. Safer immunosuppression profile compared to etoposide in septic patients (Shakoory et al., Critical Care Medicine 2016).

Malignancy-Associated HLH

Two-Step Approach:
  1. Stabilization Phase: Etoposide 75-100 mg/m² + dexamethasone + IVIG
  2. Cancer Treatment Phase: Once organ function improves
Note: Worst prognosis among HLH subtypes (20-30% 2-year survival). Must stabilize organ function before cancer-specific therapy can be tolerated. Most common undiagnosed trigger in adults.

MAS-HLH Treatment

First-Line Therapy

Methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day (max 1g) IV × 3-5 days
Cyclosporine 2-7 mg/kg/day (target trough 100-150 ng/mL)
Consider Anakinra 2-10 mg/kg/day
Rationale: Directly targets IL-1 pathway hyperactive in MAS pathophysiology. Lower mortality risk (5-10%) allows for less intensive initial approach compared to other HLH forms.

Refractory MAS-HLH

  • Etoposide: 50-100 mg/m² weekly × 1-3 doses (lower doses than primary HLH)
  • Emapalumab: 1-10 mg/kg twice weekly
  • JAK inhibitors: Ruxolitinib 0.5 mg/kg BID (pediatric)

Monitoring & Response Criteria

Response Assessment (Weekly)

Category Response Indicators
Clinical Response • Fever resolution
• Improved organ function
• Stable hemodynamics
Laboratory Response • Decreasing ferritin (>50% reduction)
• Improving cytopenias
• Normalizing triglycerides/fibrinogen

Treatment Modifications

Dose Reductions

  • Etoposide: Reduce by 25-50% for severe cytopenias
  • Age-adjusted dosing: Adults 75 mg/m², elderly 50 mg/m²
Evidence: Reduced doses in adults and elderly maintain efficacy with less toxicity (La Rosée et al., Blood 2019)

Treatment Duration

  • Primary HLH: Minimum 8 weeks, until HSCT
  • Secondary HLH: 4-8 weeks based on response
  • MAS-HLH: 2-6 weeks, taper with steroid response

Special Populations

Pediatric Modifications

  • Age-appropriate dosing calculations
  • Mandatory genetic testing
  • Early HSCT evaluation for primary HLH
  • Growth and development monitoring

Adult Considerations

  • Higher malignancy association
  • Comorbidity management
  • Reduced-intensity regimens for elderly
  • Infection prophylaxis

Pregnancy

  • Multidisciplinary team approach
  • Modified drug regimens (avoid etoposide if possible)
  • Fetal monitoring
  • Delivery planning

Evidence Base & Key References

Major Studies Supporting This Protocol

Henter et al., NEJM 2025
Comprehensive HLH review: Pre-HSCT survival improved from 73% (HLH-94) to 81% (HLH-2004). HSCT outcomes have improved significantly with better patient selection and conditioning.
Hines et al., Critical Care Medicine 2022
Histiocyte Society consensus guidelines for ICU management. Key finding: Treatment should not be delayed for diagnostic confirmation. 60% of pediatric HLH requires ICU support with 36-68% mortality.
Bergsten et al., Blood 2017
Confirmed long-term efficacy of HLH-2004 protocol with 5-year survival of 59%.
Locatelli et al., NEJM 2020
Emapalumab study: 70% overall response rate in refractory/relapsed patients. Important limitation: Only 41% survived without additional chemotherapy.
Shakoory et al., Critical Care Medicine 2016
IL-1 receptor blockade (anakinra) associated with reduced mortality in sepsis patients with MAS features.

Quality Metrics

Process Measures

  • Time to diagnosis: <24 hours
  • Time to treatment: <6 hours
  • Ferritin monitoring frequency
  • Infection surveillance compliance

Outcome Measures

  • 30-day mortality
  • ICU length of stay
  • Treatment-related toxicities
  • Long-term sequelae rates

Implementation Requirements

This protocol requires:

  • ✓ Institutional commitment to 24/7 laboratory capability
  • ✓ Multidisciplinary team including hematology, critical care, pharmacy
  • ✓ Standardized order sets for rapid treatment initiation
  • ✓ Regular protocol review based on emerging evidence
  • ✓ Staff education on HLH recognition and management
Protocol Emphasis: Early recognition, prompt treatment initiation, and individualized therapy based on disease severity and underlying triggers. Regular monitoring and adjustment are essential for optimal outcomes.
